## Taxonomic Note

*Homo lycanthropus* (Common werewolf) represents a rare and profoundly aberrant condition in which human skeletal morphology undergoes periodic and extreme lupine distortion, resulting in a hybridized form characterized by digitigrade posture, elongation of the facial bones, and hypertrophy of the appendicular skeleton.

## Etiological Debate (HBICS)

The underlying cause of Lycanthropy remains formally unestablished. Academic consensus has thus far resisted classification of the condition as preternatural, favoring instead a series of speculative naturalistic explanations. These include an unidentified viral infection, hereditary predisposition, or a complex psychosomatic disorder manifesting in somatic alteration. None of these theories has been substantiated through reproducible observation.

## Competing Hypotheses

Proposed explanations within HBICS literature include:

- A transmissible pathological agent exhibiting cyclical activation.
- An inherited condition expressed under specific environmental or physiological stressors.
- A degenerative neurological disorder accompanied by somatic hallucination.

Each theory fails to account fully for the observed periodicity, severity of transformation, or the consistent folkloric elements recorded across disparate regions.

## Folkloric Considerations

Numerous ethnographic accounts describe Lycanthropy as a supernatural curse, commonly transmitted through ritual transgression, malediction, or violent contact with an afflicted individual. Such explanations are routinely excluded from formal classification on the grounds of insufficient empirical rigor, though their persistence across cultures remains noteworthy.
